Nigerian Jollof Rice

Nigerian Jollof Rice is a rich, smoky, and deeply aromatic one-pot dish that is a staple at celebrations and family gatherings across West Africa. Made with a blended tomato and pepper base, long-grain parboiled rice, and fragrant spices, it is beloved for its bold colour and unforgettable taste, especially when served with fried chicken and sweet fried plantain.

Recipe Details

Prep Time Cook Time Total Time Servings Difficulty
20 minutes 55 minutes 1 hour 15 minutes 6 people Medium

Ingredients

  • 3 cups long-grain parboiled rice
  • 6 large ripe tomatoes, blended
  • 3 red bell peppers (tatashe), blended
  • 2 to 3 scotch bonnet peppers, blended
  • 1 large onion — half sliced, half blended
  • 1/2 cup vegetable or sunflower oil
  • 2 cups chicken stock or water
  • 2 teaspoons curry powder
  • 1 teaspoon dried thyme
  • 2 seasoning cubes (Maggi or Knorr)
  • Salt to taste
  • Bay leaves (optional, for extra aroma)
  • To serve: fried chicken and sweet fried plantain

Instructions

  1. Blend the pepper base: Combine the tomatoes, red bell peppers, scotch bonnet peppers, and the blended half of the onion in a blender. Blend until very smooth. Set aside.
  2. Fry the tomato blend: Heat the oil in a large, heavy-bottomed pot over medium-high heat. Add the sliced onion and fry until soft and golden. Pour in the blended tomato mixture and fry, stirring regularly, for 20 to 25 minutes until the raw smell is gone, the colour deepens to a rich red, and the oil floats to the top.
  3. Season the base: Add the curry powder, dried thyme, crumbled seasoning cubes, salt, and bay leaves if using. Stir well and cook for a further 2 minutes to allow the spices to bloom in the oil.
  4. Add stock and rice: Pour in the chicken stock. Taste and adjust the salt. Wash the parboiled rice under cold water until the water runs clear, then add it to the pot. Stir everything together to make sure the rice is evenly coated in the sauce.
  5. Cook on low heat: Bring the pot to a boil, then reduce the heat to the lowest setting possible. Cover the pot tightly — first with a sheet of aluminium foil, then with the lid on top — to trap all the steam inside. Cook for 25 to 30 minutes without lifting the lid.
  6. Check and finish: Remove the lid and foil, then gently stir from the bottom upwards. If the rice is cooked through and the liquid has been absorbed, it is ready. For the authentic smoky "party jollof" flavour, leave the heat on the lowest setting for an extra 3 to 5 minutes until you hear a faint crackling at the bottom of the pot.

Tips and Notes

  • Use parboiled rice — it holds its shape and absorbs the tomato base beautifully without turning mushy.
  • The longer you fry the tomato blend in Step 2, the richer and less acidic your jollof will taste. Do not rush this step.
  • Always cover with foil before the lid — this is the traditional technique that traps steam and ensures every grain of rice cooks perfectly.
  • Serve with fried plantain, coleslaw, moi moi, or grilled chicken for a complete Nigerian party spread.
  • Leftover jollof rice reheats well — just add a small splash of water and cover the pan on low heat for a few minutes.
